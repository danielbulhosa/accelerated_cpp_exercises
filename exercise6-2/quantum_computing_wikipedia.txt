   Quantum computing is the use of [9]quantum phenomena such as
   [10]superposition and [11]entanglement to perform
   [12]computation. Computers that perform quantum computations
   are known as quantum computers.^[13][1]^:I-5 Quantum computers
   are believed to be able to solve certain [14]computational
   problems, such as [15]integer factorization (which underlies
   [16]RSA encryption), substantially faster than classical
   computers. The study of quantum computing is a subfield of
   [17]quantum information science.

   Quantum computing began in the early 1980s, when physicist
   [18]Paul Benioff proposed a [19]quantum mechanical model of the
   [20]Turing machine.^[21][2] [22]Richard Feynman and [23]Yuri
   Manin later suggested that a quantum computer had the potential
   to simulate things that a [24]classical computer could
   not.^[25][3]^[26][4] In 1994, [27]Peter Shor developed a
   quantum [28]algorithm for [29]factoring integers that had the
   potential to decrypt [30]RSA-encrypted communications.^[31][5]
   Despite ongoing experimental progress since the late 1990s,
   most researchers believe that "[32]fault-tolerant quantum
   computing [is] still a rather distant dream."^[33][6] In recent
   years, investment into quantum computing research has increased
   in both the public and private sector.^[34][7]^[35][8] On 23
   October 2019, [36]Google AI, in partnership with the U.S.
   National Aeronautics and Space Administration ([37]NASA),
   claimed to have performed a quantum computation that is
   [38]infeasible on any classical computer.^[39][9]

   There are several models of quantum computers (or rather,
   quantum computing systems), including the [40]quantum circuit
   model, [41]quantum Turing machine, [42]adiabatic quantum
   computer, [43]one-way quantum computer, and various [44]quantum
   cellular automata. The most widely used model is the
   [45]quantum circuit. Quantum circuits are based on the quantum
   bit, or "[46]qubit", which is somewhat analogous to the [47]bit
   in classical computation. Qubits can be in a 1 or 0 [48]quantum
   state, or they can be in a [49]superposition of the 1 and 0
   states. However, when qubits are measured the result of the
   measurement is always either a 0 or a 1; the [50]probabilities
   of these two outcomes depend on the [51]quantum state that the
   qubits were in immediately prior to the measurement.

   Progress towards building a physical quantum computer focuses
   on technologies such as [52]transmons, [53]ion traps and
   [54]topological quantum computers, which aim to create
   high-quality [55]qubits.^[56][1]^:2–13 These qubits may be
   designed differently, depending on the full quantum computer's
   computing model, whether [57]quantum logic gates, [58]quantum
   annealing, or [59]adiabatic quantum computation. There are
   currently a number of significant obstacles in the way of
   constructing useful quantum computers. In particular, it is
   difficult to maintain the quantum states of qubits as they
   suffer from [60]quantum decoherence and [61]state fidelity.
   Quantum computers therefore require [62]error
   correction.^[63][10]^[64][11]

   Any [65]computational problem that can be solved by a classical
   computer can also be solved by a quantum computer.^[66][12]
   Conversely, any problem that can be solved by a quantum
   computer can also be solved by a classical computer, at least
   in principle given enough time. In other words, quantum
   computers obey the [67]Church–Turing thesis. While this means
   that quantum computers provide no additional advantages over
   classical computers in terms of [68]computability, [69]quantum
   algorithms for certain problems have significantly lower
   [70]time complexities than corresponding known classical
   algorithms. Notably, quantum computers are believed to be able
   to quickly solve certain problems that no classical computer
   could solve in any feasible amount of time—a feat known as
   "[71]quantum supremacy." The study of the [72]computational
   complexity of problems with respect to quantum computers is
   known as [73]quantum complexity theory.

   The prevailing model of quantum computation describes the
   computation in terms of a network of [103]quantum logic
   gates.^[104][13] This model can be thought of as an abstract
   [105]linear-algebraic generalization of a [106]classical
   circuit. Since this circuit model obeys [107]quantum mechanics,
   a quantum computer capable of efficiently running these
   circuits is believed to be physically realizable.

   In summary, a quantum computation can be described as a network
   of quantum logic gates and measurements. However, any
   measurement can be deferred to the end of a quantum
   computation, though this deferment may come at a computational
   cost, so most [119]quantum circuits depict a network consisting
   only of quantum logic gates and no measurements.

    [119] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_circuit

   Any quantum computation (which is, in the above formalism, any
   unitary matrix over
   [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
   displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <mi>n</mi> </mstyle>
   </mrow> <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle
   n}</annotation> </semantics> :MATH]
   n qubits) can be represented as a network of quantum logic
   gates from a fairly small family of gates. A choice of gate
   family that enables this construction is known as a
   [120]universal gate set, since a computer that can run such
   circuits is a [121]universal quantum computer. One common such
   set includes all single-qubit gates as well as the CNOT gate
   from above. This means any quantum computation can be performed
   by executing a sequence of single-qubit gates together with
   CNOT gates. Though this gate set is infinite, it can be
   replaced with a finite gate set by appealing to the
   [122]Solovay-Kitaev theorem. The representation of multiple
   qubits can be shown as Qsphere.

Quantum algorithms[[123]edit]

    [123] https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Quantum_computing&action=edit&section=3

   Main article: [124]Quantum algorithm

    [124] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_algorithm

   Progress in finding quantum algorithms typically focuses on
   this quantum circuit model, though exceptions like the
   [125]quantum adiabatic algorithm exist. Quantum algorithms can
   be roughly categorized by the type of speedup achieved over
   corresponding classical algorithms.^[126][14]

    [125] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adiabatic_quantum_computation
     [14] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computational_problem

   Quantum algorithms that offer more than a polynomial speedup
   over the best known classical algorithm include [127]Shor's
   algorithm for factoring and the related quantum algorithms for
   computing [128]discrete logarithms, solving [129]Pell's
   equation, and more generally solving the [130]hidden subgroup
   problem for abelian finite groups.^[131][14] These algorithms
   depend on the primitive of the [132]quantum Fourier transform.
   No mathematical proof has been found that shows that an equally
   fast classical algorithm cannot be discovered, although this is
   considered unlikely.^[133][15] Certain oracle problems like
   [134]Simon's problem and the [135]Bernstein–Vazirani problem do
   give provable speedups, though this is in the [136]quantum
   query model, which is a restricted model where lower bounds are
   much easier to prove, and doesn't necessarily translate to
   speedups for practical problems.

    [127] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shor's_algorithm
    [128] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discrete_logarithm
    [129] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pell's_equation
    [130] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hidden_subgroup_problem
     [14] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computational_problem
    [132] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_Fourier_transform
     [15] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer_factorization
    [134] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simon's_problem
    [135] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bernstein–Vazirani_algorithm
    [136] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_complexity_theory#quantum_query_complexity

   Other problems, including the simulation of quantum physical
   processes from chemistry and solid state physics, the
   approximation of certain [137]Jones polynomials, and the
   [138]quantum algorithm for linear systems of equations have
   quantum algorithms appearing to give super-polynomial speedups
   and are [139]BQP-complete. Because these problems are
   BQP-complete, an equally fast classical algorithm for them
   would imply that no quantum algorithm gives a super-polynomial
   speedup, which is believed to be unlikely.^[140][16]

    [137] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jones_polynomial
    [138] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_algorithm_for_linear_systems_of_equations
    [139] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BQP

   Some quantum algorithms, like [141]Grover's algorithm and
   [142]amplitude amplification, give polynomial speedups over
   corresponding classical algorithms.^[143][14] Though these
   algorithms give comparably modest quadratic speedup, they are
   widely applicable and thus give speedups for a wide range of
   problems.^[144][17] Many examples of provable quantum speedups
   for query problems are related to Grover's algorithm, including
   [145]Brassard, Høyer, and Tapp's algorithm for finding
   collisions in two-to-one functions,^[146][18] which uses
   Grover's algorithm, and Farhi, Goldstone, and Gutmann's
   algorithm for evaluating NAND trees,^[147][19] which is a
   variant of the search problem.

Cryptography[[149]edit]

    [149] https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Quantum_computing&action=edit&section=5

   Main articles: [150]Quantum cryptography and [151]Post-quantum
   cryptography

    [150] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_cryptography
    [151] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Post-quantum_cryptography

   A notable application of quantum computation is for
   [152]attacks on cryptographic systems that are currently in
   use. [153]Integer factorization, which underpins the security
   of [154]public key cryptographic systems, is believed to be
   computationally infeasible with an ordinary computer for large
   integers if they are the product of few [155]prime numbers
   (e.g., products of two 300-digit primes).^[156][20] By
   comparison, a quantum computer could efficiently solve this
   problem using [157]Shor's algorithm to find its factors. This
   ability would allow a quantum computer to break many of the
   [158]cryptographic systems in use today, in the sense that
   there would be a [159]polynomial time (in the number of digits
   of the integer) algorithm for solving the problem. In
   particular, most of the popular [160]public key ciphers are
   based on the difficulty of factoring integers or the
   [161]discrete logarithm problem, both of which can be solved by
   Shor's algorithm. In particular, the [162]RSA,
   [163]Diffie–Hellman, and [164]elliptic curve Diffie–Hellman
   algorithms could be broken. These are used to protect secure
   Web pages, encrypted email, and many other types of data.
   Breaking these would have significant ramifications for
   electronic privacy and security.

   Identifying cryptographic systems that may be secure against
   quantum algorithms is an actively researched topic under the
   field of [165]post-quantum cryptography. ^[166][21]^[167][22]
   Some public-key algorithms are based on problems other than the
   integer factorization and discrete logarithm problems to which
   Shor's algorithm applies, like the [168]McEliece cryptosystem
   based on a problem in [169]coding theory.^[170][21]^[171][23]
   [172]Lattice-based cryptosystems are also not known to be
   broken by quantum computers, and finding a polynomial time
   algorithm for solving the [173]dihedral [174]hidden subgroup
   problem, which would break many lattice based cryptosystems, is
   a well-studied open problem.^[175][24] It has been proven that
   applying Grover's algorithm to break a [176]symmetric (secret
   key) algorithm by brute force requires time equal to roughly
   2^n/2 invocations of the underlying cryptographic algorithm,
   compared with roughly 2^n in the classical case,^[177][25]
   meaning that symmetric key lengths are effectively halved:
   AES-256 would have the same security against an attack using
   Grover's algorithm that AES-128 has against classical
   brute-force search (see [178]Key size).

   Problems that can be addressed with [183]Grover's algorithm
   have the following properties:^[[184]citation needed]
    1. There is no searchable structure in the collection of
       possible answers,
    2. The number of possible answers to check is the same as the
       number of inputs to the algorithm, and
    3. There exists a boolean function which evaluates each input
       and determines whether it is the correct answer

    [183] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grover's_algorithm
    [184] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed

   For problems with all these properties, the running time of
   [185]Grover's algorithm on a quantum computer will scale as the
   square root of the number of inputs (or elements in the
   database), as opposed to the linear scaling of classical
   algorithms. A general class of problems to which [186]Grover's
   algorithm can be applied^[187][27] is [188]Boolean
   satisfiability problem. In this instance, the database through
   which the algorithm is iterating is that of all possible
   answers. An example (and possible) application of this is a
   [189]password cracker that attempts to guess the password or
   secret key for an [190]encrypted file or system. [191]Symmetric
   ciphers such as [192]Triple DES and [193]AES are particularly
   vulnerable to this kind of attack.^[[194]citation needed] This
   application of quantum computing is a major interest of
   government agencies.^[195][28]

Quantum annealing and adiabatic optimization[[208]edit]

    [208] https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Quantum_computing&action=edit&section=8

   [209]Quantum annealing or [210]Adiabatic quantum computation
   relies on the adiabatic theorem to undertake calculations. A
   system is placed in the ground state for a simple Hamiltonian,
   which is slowly evolved to a more complicated Hamiltonian whose
   ground state represents the solution to the problem in
   question. The adiabatic theorem states that if the evolution is
   slow enough the system will stay in its ground state at all
   times through the process.

Quantum decoherence[[256]edit]

    [256] https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Quantum_computing&action=edit&section=12

   Main article: [257]Quantum decoherence

    [257] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_decoherence

   One of the greatest challenges involved with constructing
   quantum computers is controlling or removing [258]quantum
   decoherence. This usually means isolating the system from its
   environment as interactions with the external world cause the
   system to decohere. However, other sources of decoherence also
   exist. Examples include the quantum gates, and the lattice
   vibrations and background thermonuclear spin of the physical
   system used to implement the qubits. Decoherence is
   irreversible, as it is effectively non-unitary, and is usually
   something that should be highly controlled, if not avoided.
   Decoherence times for candidate systems in particular, the
   transverse relaxation time T[2] (for [259]NMR and [260]MRI
   technology, also called the dephasing time), typically range
   between nanoseconds and seconds at low temperature.^[261][48]
   Currently, some quantum computers require their qubits to be
   cooled to 20 millikelvins in order to prevent significant
   decoherence.^[262][49] A 2020 study argues that [263]ionizing
   radiation such as [264]cosmic rays can nevertheless cause
   certain systems to decohere within milliseconds.^[265][50]

    [258] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_decoherence
      [2] https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Quantum_computing&action=edit
    [259] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_magnetic_resonance
    [260] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MRI
     [48] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_state
     [49] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_superposition
    [263] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ionizing_radiation
    [264] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_rays
     [50] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability

   As a result, time-consuming tasks may render some quantum
   algorithms inoperable, as maintaining the state of qubits for a
   long enough duration will eventually corrupt the
   superpositions.^[266][51]

     [51] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_state

   These issues are more difficult for optical approaches as the
   timescales are orders of magnitude shorter and an often-cited
   approach to overcoming them is optical [267]pulse shaping.
   Error rates are typically proportional to the ratio of
   operating time to decoherence time, hence any operation must be
   completed much more quickly than the decoherence time.

    [267] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulse_shaping

   As described in the [268]Quantum threshold theorem, if the
   error rate is small enough, it is thought to be possible to use
   quantum error correction to suppress errors and decoherence.
   This allows the total calculation time to be longer than the
   decoherence time if the error correction scheme can correct
   errors faster than decoherence introduces them. An often cited
   figure for the required error rate in each gate for
   fault-tolerant computation is 10^−3, assuming the noise is
   depolarizing.

    [268] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_threshold_theorem

   Meeting this scalability condition is possible for a wide range
   of systems. However, the use of error correction brings with it
   the cost of a greatly increased number of required qubits. The
   number required to factor integers using Shor's algorithm is
   still polynomial, and thought to be between L and L^2, where L
   is the number of qubits in the number to be factored; error
   correction algorithms would inflate this figure by an
   additional factor of L. For a 1000-bit number, this implies a
   need for about 10^4 bits without error correction.^[269][52]
   With error correction, the figure would rise to about 10^7
   bits. Computation time is about L^2 or about 10^7 steps and at
   1 MHz, about 10 seconds.

     [52] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transmon

   A very different approach to the stability-decoherence problem
   is to create a [270]topological quantum computer with
   [271]anyons, [272]quasi-particles used as threads and relying
   on [273]braid theory to form stable logic
   gates.^[274][53]^[275][54]

    [270] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_quantum_computer
    [271] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anyon
    [272] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasi-particle
    [273] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Braid_theory
     [53] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapped_ion_quantum_computer
     [54] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_quantum_computer

   Physicist [276]Mikhail Dyakonov has expressed skepticism of
   quantum computing as follows:

    [276] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Dyakonov

          "So the number of continuous parameters describing the
          state of such a useful quantum computer at any given
          moment must be... about 10^300... Could we ever learn to
          control the more than 10^300 continuously variable
          parameters defining the quantum state of such a system?
          My answer is simple. No, never."^[277][55]^[278][56]

Relation to computability and complexity theory[[351]edit]

    [351] https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Quantum_computing&action=edit&section=16

Computability theory[[352]edit]

    [352] https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Quantum_computing&action=edit&section=17

   See also: [353]Computability theory

    [353] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computability_theory

   Any [354]computational problem solvable by a classical computer
   is also solvable by a quantum computer.^[355][82] Intuitively,
   this is because it is believed that all physical phenomena,
   including the operation of classical computers, can be
   described using [356]quantum mechanics, which underlies the
   operation of quantum computers.

    [354] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computational_problem
    [356] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics

   Conversely, any problem solvable by a quantum computer is also
   solvable by a classical computer; or more formally, any quantum
   computer can be simulated by a [357]Turing machine. In other
   words, quantum computers provide no additional power over
   classical computers in terms of [358]computability. This means
   that quantum computers cannot solve [359]undecidable problems
   like the [360]halting problem and the existence of quantum
   computers does not disprove the [361]Church–Turing
   thesis.^[362][83]

    [357] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turing_machine
    [358] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computability
    [359] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Undecidable_problem
    [360] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halting_problem
    [361] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Church–Turing_thesis

   As of yet, quantum computers do not satisfy the [363]strong
   Church thesis. While hypothetical machines have been realized,
   a universal quantum computer has yet to be physically
   constructed. The strong version of Church's thesis requires a
   physical computer, and therefore there is no quantum computer
   that yet satisfies the strong Church thesis.

    [363] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Church–Turing_thesis

Quantum complexity theory[[364]edit]

    [364] https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Quantum_computing&action=edit&section=18

   Main article: [365]Quantum complexity theory

    [365] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_complexity_theory

   While quantum computers cannot solve any problems that
   classical computers cannot already solve, it is suspected that
   they can solve many problems faster than classical computers.
   For instance, it is known that quantum computers can
   efficiently [366]factor integers, while this is not believed to
   be the case for classical computers. However, the capacity of
   quantum computers to accelerate classical algorithms has rigid
   upper bounds, and the overwhelming majority of classical
   calculations cannot be accelerated by the use of quantum
   computers.^[367][84]

    [366] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer_factorization

   The class of [368]problems that can be efficiently solved by a
   quantum computer with bounded error is called [369]BQP, for
   "bounded error, quantum, polynomial time". More formally, BQP
   is the class of problems that can be solved by a
   polynomial-time [370]quantum Turing machine with error
   probability of at most 1/3. As a class of probabilistic
   problems, BQP is the quantum counterpart to [371]BPP ("bounded
   error, probabilistic, polynomial time"), the class of problems
   that can be solved by polynomial-time [372]probabilistic Turing
   machines with bounded error.^[373][85] It is known that BPP
   [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
   displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <mo>⊆</mo> </mstyle>
   </mrow> <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle
   \subseteq }</annotation> </semantics> :MATH]
   \subseteq BQP and is widely suspected that BQP
   [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
   displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <mo>⊈</mo> </mstyle>
   </mrow> <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle
   \nsubseteq }</annotation> </semantics> :MATH]
   \nsubseteq BPP, which intuitively would mean that quantum
   computers are more powerful than classical computers in terms
   of time complexity.^[374][86]
   The suspected relationship of BQP to several classical
   complexity classes.^[375][87]

    [368] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computational_problem
    [369] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BQP
    [370] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_Turing_machine
    [371] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bounded-error_probabilistic_polynomial
    [372] https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probabilistic_Turing_machine

   The exact relationship of BQP to [376]P, [377]NP, and
   [378]PSPACE is not known. However, it is known that P
   [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
   displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <mo>⊆</mo> </mstyle>
   </mrow> <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle
   \subseteq }</annotation> </semantics> :MATH]
   \subseteq BQP
   [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
   displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <mo>⊆</mo> </mstyle>
   </mrow> <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle
   \subseteq }</annotation> </semantics> :MATH]
   \subseteq PSPACE; that is, all problems that can be efficiently
   solved by a deterministic classical computer can also be
   efficiently solved by a quantum computer, and all problems that
   can be efficiently solved by a quantum computer can also be
   solved by a deterministic classical computer with polynomial
   space resources. It is further suspected that BQP is a strict
   superset of P, meaning there are problems that are efficiently
   solvable by quantum computers that are not efficiently solvable
   by deterministic classical computers. For instance,
   [379]integer factorization and the [380]discrete logarithm
   problem are known to be in BQP and are suspected to be outside
   of P. On the relationship of BQP to NP, little is known beyond
   the fact that some NP problems that are believed not to be in P
   are also in BQP (integer factorization and the discrete
   logarithm problem are both in NP, for example). It is suspected
   that NP

   \nsubseteq BQP; that is, it is believed that there are
   efficiently checkable problems that are not efficiently
   solvable by a quantum computer. As a direct consequence of this
   belief, it is also suspected that BQP is disjoint from the
   class of [381]NP-complete problems (if an NP-complete problem
   were in BQP, then it would follow from [382]NP-hardness that
   all problems in NP are in BQP).^[383][88]

   It has been speculated that further advances in physics could
   lead to even faster computers. For instance, it has been shown
   that a non-local hidden variable quantum computer based on
   [387]Bohmian Mechanics could implement a search of an
   [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
   displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <mi>N</mi> </mstyle>
   </mrow> <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle
   N}</annotation> </semantics> :MATH]
   N -item database in at most
   [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle

   {\displaystyle O({\sqrt[{3}]{N}})} steps, a slight speedup over
   [388]Grover's algorithm, which runs in
   [MATH: <semantics> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <mstyle
   displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0"> <mi>O</mi> <mo
   stretchy="false">(</mo> <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD"> <msqrt>

   O({\sqrt {N}}) steps. Note, however, that neither search method
   would allow quantum computers to solve [389]NP-complete
   problems in polynomial time.^[390][89] Theories of [391]quantum
   gravity, such as [392]M-theory and [393]loop quantum gravity,
   may allow even faster computers to be built. However, defining
   computation in these theories is an open problem due to the
   [394]problem of time; that is, within these physical theories
   there is currently no obvious way to describe what it means for
   an observer to submit input to a computer at one point in time
   and then receive output at a later point in
   time.^[395][90]^[396][91]
